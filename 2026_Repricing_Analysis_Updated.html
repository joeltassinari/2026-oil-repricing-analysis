Since opening 2026 at $57/bbl, WTI crude has undergone a violent 96% appreciation, surging to $112/bbl by early April as the Hormuz disruption intensified. This rapid ascent has created a profound valuation gap between physical commodity reality and equity market sentiment. While the gold WTI line has nearly doubled, the equity-implied oil prices for Airlines and Industrials have remained virtually stagnant — signaling a dangerous level of complacency. These sectors are effectively trading on stale energy assumptions, leaving them with an unpriced exposure of $58/bbl (Airlines) and $60/bbl (Industrials). Unless WTI undergoes rapid mean reversion, these sectors face significant catch-up risk — earnings multiples must eventually compress to reflect eroded margins.

The geopolitical backdrop as of early April remains acutely uncertain. In a primetime address on April 2, President Trump declared that the month-long military campaign in Iran is "nearing completion," claiming the Iranian Navy and Air Force have been effectively neutralized. Yet in the same address, he threatened intensified strikes over the coming weeks targeting missile production infrastructure — a direct contradiction that rattled oil markets overnight. He simultaneously suggested secret negotiations with an emerging Iranian leadership are making progress, while threatening to reduce Iran to rubble if nuclear development continues. U.S. allies were told to secure the Strait themselves if they want continued oil access, signaling an impending American withdrawal. Markets interpreted the speech as every scenario simultaneously: resolution, escalation, and abandonment — which is precisely why the forward simulation fan is as wide as it is.

In contrast, the Software sector's implied price over-corrected sharply upward, peaking near $160/bbl in February before partially retracing. This decoupling reflects macro stressors beyond oil — AI capex uncertainty and tariff fears drove additional multiple compression unrelated to energy fundamentals. Data Centers (now including Google alongside Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta) show a revised gap of approximately $20/bbl — meaningfully wider once Google's 2.4x multiple compression is incorporated into the average.

Looking forward, the Monte Carlo simulation establishes a median year-end expectation of — WTI under the Base scenario — elevated but below today's spot, implying partial Hormuz normalization. The distribution is unusually wide: the P95 upper bound reaches —, a level that would represent a historically unprecedented sustained oil price and a catastrophic repricing event for sectors like Airlines that have yet to absorb even the current $112 reality into their equity multiples. The P5 lower bound implies meaningful relief — but only if military de-escalation materializes faster than current signals suggest.

1 · Context & The Repricing Gap
Geopolitical backdrop · EV/EBITDA methodology · Rate isolation · Software anomaly
▶

This analysis was constructed against a live geopolitical disruption rather than a hypothetical one. As of April 3, 2026, WTI crude settled at approximately $112/barrel — representing a 96% increase since the beginning of the year — following military action in the Middle East and a material reduction in petroleum shipments through the Strait of Hormuz. The EIA's March 10, 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook revised its 2026 Brent forecast upward by 37% relative to the prior month's publication, while simultaneously projecting U.S. crude production at 13.6 mmbpd, an all-time record.

This coexistence of record domestic output and explosive price appreciation is the Fortress America paradox at the center of this analysis. Marginal pricing on global crude markets means that U.S. production volume has no bearing on the domestic cost of capital — oil is priced globally, and the cost of money follows the oil price regardless of where the barrels are drilled.

What the repricing gap measures: The divergence between the oil price implied by current equity valuations and the actual WTI spot price. A positive gap means equities are discounting oil as though the disruption hasn't happened. This is the "stale assumptions" problem — sector multiples haven't compressed to reflect the new cost-of-capital reality.

Step 1 — Baseline: January 2, 2026 is the pre-conflict baseline date. WTI closed at $57.33 that day. FactSet NTM EV/EBITDA multiples for JETS, XLI, IGV, META, AMZN, MSFT, and GOOGL (Alphabet A) on that date anchor all calculations.

Step 2 — Implied WACC from EV/EBITDA: Using a Gordon Growth perpetuity approximation with g = 5% (appropriate for EBITDA-level, not equity-level, growth):

WACC = (1 + g) / EV/EBITDA + g

The delta in WACC between the baseline date and any subsequent date captures how much the market's implied cost of capital has moved.

Step 3 — Rate isolation: The 10Y Treasury moved from 4.19% (Jan 2) to 4.31% (Apr 2) — a 12bps independent move. This must be stripped from the total WACC delta to isolate the oil-specific component. Per sector: Rate WACC Δ = ΔRate × (1 − D/(D+E) × 21% tax rate). The residual is the oil-specific repricing.

Step 4 — Back to oil: Oil-specific WACC Δ ÷ sector sensitivity coefficient (bps per $10/bbl) × $10 + $57.33 baseline = implied oil price. The gap to actual WTI ($112) is the unpriced exposure.

NTM vs. LTM toggle: The chart offers both Next-Twelve-Months (forward consensus) and Last-Twelve-Months (trailing actuals) EV/EBITDA. For Airlines and Industrials the implied oil gap is virtually identical on both bases (~$58 and ~$60 respectively) — confirming the gap is not an artifact of analyst forecast assumptions. For Software, LTM shows a small positive gap ($9) vs. NTM over-correction (−$26), because forward estimates were cut more aggressively than trailing actuals reflect. For Data Centers, LTM shows a wider gap ($34) than NTM ($20) for the opposite reason.

The software anomaly: IGV's implied oil sits at ~$138 — above actual WTI. EV/EBITDA multiples compressed more than oil fundamentals alone explain. AI capex uncertainty, hardware tariff risk, and broader growth-multiple contraction drove additional compression through non-oil channels. This is explicitly acknowledged and does not undermine the thesis — it demonstrates that the model is not mechanically biased upward. The sectors with the largest positive gaps (airlines, industrials) are precisely the ones with direct operating cost exposure to oil, not growth-stock concerns.

3 · Forward Simulation — Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process
OU mean reversion · Volatility calibration · Markov transitions · 2026 scope
▶

The forward simulation covers April 3 to December 31, 2026 — 9 monthly steps, 2,000 paths. This is explicitly a 2026 analysis. The simulation does not extrapolate beyond year-end.

Stochastic process — Ornstein-Uhlenbeck on log price: Unlike Geometric Brownian Motion (which has no equilibrium pull), the OU process incorporates the empirically observed tendency of commodity prices to revert toward fundamental levels within regimes. The discrete approximation used here:

logP(t+dt) = logP(t) + κ·(logTarget − logP(t))·dt + σ·√dt·Z

Where κ is the speed of mean reversion, Target is the regime-specific price anchor, σ is realized volatility, and Z ~ N(0,1). This is the standard formulation for commodity price simulation in financial research.

Reversion speed (κ) calibration: κ = 0.40 for Regime 1 (fast reversion — resolution scenario prices normalize relatively quickly), κ = 0.30 for Regime 2 (moderate — elevated plateau with gradual easing), κ = 0.18 for Regime 3 (slow — entrenched disruption, prices remain near current levels). These values imply that Regime 1 paths close approximately 60% of the gap to target over 24 months; Regime 3 closes approximately 30%.

Volatility: 50% annualized realized volatility, calibrated to the sustained CBOE OVX range observed during the 2022 Ukraine disruption period (~47–52% annualized). The current OVX reading of 91.8% (April 1, 2026) substantially overstates expected realized volatility because it includes a large variance risk premium — the market is paying for tail insurance, not predicting that prices will realize 92% vol. The 50% figure is the appropriate realized-vol anchor. Volatility decays linearly to regime-specific floors over 9 months (R1: 28%, R2: 35%, R3: 40%). Price floor: $56.40 (2026 intra-year trough, January 7). Price ceiling: $195.

Regime transitions: Symmetric Markov matrix with 80% self-transition probability per monthly step. Each regime transitions to others proportionally to their initial weights. This produces a stationary distribution that converges to the initial weights over time — the symmetric assumption avoids embedding directional bias into the dynamics beyond what the initial weights already express.

Path count and convergence: 2,000 paths is standard for financial Monte Carlo at this complexity level. Convergence testing shows that key statistics (median path, P5/P95 percentiles) stabilize at approximately 1,500 paths. The 2,000-path count provides a margin of safety without material computational cost.

4 · WACC Sensitivity Derivations
Rate transmission channel · IATA fuel cost · BLS energy intensity · Damodaran leverage · Full arithmetic
▶

WACC sensitivity coefficients (bps per $10/barrel oil increase) are derived from three compounding channels. All inputs are verified against primary sources. Derived figures are explicitly labeled.

Channel 1 — Rate channel (universal, all sectors): Oil shocks raise headline CPI, which raises inflation expectations, which causes the Federal Reserve to maintain or tighten rates, which raises the risk-free rate embedded in every sector's WACC. SF Fed Economic Letter (December 2025) documents this transmission: a 3% oil price surprise raises 2-year Treasury yields by 4.5bps in the current regime — more than 3× the pre-2021 sensitivity, representing a structural break post-2022. Derivation: at $70 base price, a $10 increase = 14.3% move. Linear extrapolation: 4.5 × (14.3/3.0) ≈ 21bps gross rate impact. Discounted to ~15bps to reflect non-linearity at large price moves. This is the floor sensitivity — all sectors receive at least this component.

Channel 2 — Operating cost channel (Airlines, Industrials): Direct energy costs embedded in P&L. Quantified using BLS Total Factor Productivity data (Table 11, December 2025 release, covering 2024 data) and IATA industry statistics.

Channel 3 — Leverage amplification: Higher debt costs and refinancing risk compound the rate channel. Leverage ratios from Damodaran January 2026 sector data.

5 · Equity Translation, Limitations & Full Bibliography
DCF mechanics · Key caveats · What this model does not capture · Primary sources
▶

Equity translation mechanics: WACC changes are converted to equity value impact using a standardized DCF sensitivity. A company with long-run growth g = 8% and a terminal multiple of 25× has an effective equity duration of approximately 12 years. At this duration, 100bps WACC expansion reduces equity value by approximately 17%.

Equity Impact ≈ −17% per +100bps WACC · Capped at ±100%

This is a pure discount rate effect — it assumes earnings estimates are unchanged. In practice, airlines and industrials face a second hit: higher fuel and energy costs will directly compress earnings estimates, amplifying the equity headwind beyond what WACC alone implies. The equity impact shown here is therefore a conservative (understated) estimate of the real headwind for these sectors. Paths exceeding ~500bps WACC expansion imply financial distress rather than valuation compression; treat Bear scenario P95 tails as stress scenarios, not base cases.

Key limitations:

  • WACC sensitivities are derived estimates, not regression-estimated from time-series data. A more rigorous approach would panel-regress sector WACCs on lagged oil prices. The derivations are transparent and defensible but involve judgment at the aggregation step.
  • The EV/EBITDA → implied oil inversion assumes Gordon Growth applies and that all multiple compression is discount-rate-driven. Earnings revisions compress multiples independently — the implied oil gap may understate the true unpriced exposure.
  • Polymarket weights are 3-month touch probabilities, not 9-month regime residence probabilities. The conversion to regime weights applies a conservatism discount — different reasonable conversion approaches shift weights by several percentage points.
  • BLS TFP data has a 12-month publication lag. December 2025 release covers 2024. 2025 energy intensity data is unavailable until December 2026.
  • Data center power-crude linkage is the least-verified sensitivity component. The electricity → natural gas → crude price chain is real but the pass-through coefficient has not been independently estimated for this document.
  • The model does not capture hedging activity, demand destruction feedback, or second-order macroeconomic effects of sustained high oil prices on GDP and corporate earnings broadly.
